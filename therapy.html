Therapy & rehabilitation

Therapy for Spastic Diplegia

Spastic diplegia, meaning stiff, tight legs that make standing and walking hard, is one of the most common patterns in cerebral palsy. Physical therapy is the main way children gain strength, mobility and independence, and the evidence keeps returning to one idea above all: active, goal-directed practice, done often, beats passive treatment.

Not medical advice. There is a great deal that can help, and the well-studied principles of therapy for spastic diplegia, namely active, goal-directed, early and individualised, transfer well, even though there is no COPB2-specific protocol yet. Because COPB2 is genetic and may behave differently from the brain-injury cerebral palsy this evidence comes from, a qualified physiotherapist should tailor everything to your child and confirm nothing here is unsuitable, for example before vigorous strengthening or surgery, and, for the bone form (OPDD), how to load bones safely. This page is general information for education, not a treatment plan.

The core idea

The One Idea That Makes Therapy Work

Across every high-quality review, the therapies that genuinely improve movement share one mechanism: the child does the movement themselves, practises it a lot, and works toward a real goal they care about, such as climbing the stairs, keeping up at the playground, or walking to class. Active, repeated, meaningful practice is what drives the brain to rewire (what scientists call experience-dependent neuroplasticity).

The flip side is just as important. Passive, hands-on treatment, where a therapist moves the child or tries to “normalise” muscle tone while the child stays passive, does not work. A useful test: if a therapy is mostly something done to your child rather than something your child actively does, it is fair to ask for the evidence.

The field’s reference map is the Novak “traffic-light” review, which grades cerebral-palsy treatments green (do it), yellow (probably, and measure it) or red (don’t): Novak et al., 2020 (PubMed).

What works

What the Evidence Supports

A quick map of therapy approaches for spastic diplegia, grouped by how well research supports them. “Supported” means the best available evidence backs doing it, not that it is a cure.

Do: Supported

  • Goal-directed, task-specific practice
  • Progressive strength training
  • Treadmill (± body-weight support) & gait training
  • Botulinum toxin + active therapy; serial casting
  • Selective dorsal rhizotomy (for selected ambulant diplegia)
  • Ankle-foot orthoses, matched to the problem
  • Hip surveillance; weight-bearing for bone
  • Early detection & early intervention; parent coaching

Maybe: Try & Measure

  • Functional electrical stimulation (foot-drop)
  • Robotic gait training (mixed results)
  • Aquatic therapy / hydrotherapy
  • Hippotherapy (mainly posture & trunk)
  • Adapted or FES cycling
  • Standing programs (for bone & hips)

Question: Weak or No Benefit

  • Classic passive NDT / Bobath
  • Passive stretching alone (for contractures)
  • Sensory integration (for motor skills)
  • Suit therapy (Therasuit / Adeli)
  • Hyperbaric oxygen; craniosacral therapy
  • Conductive education, Vojta (for motor outcomes)

Grades summarised from Novak et al., 2020 and the Cochrane and guideline reviews cited below. The cerebral-palsy evidence base is mostly low-to-moderate quality, so these are best estimates, not guarantees.

The building blocks

The Therapies, Explained

For a child with spastic diplegia, a good plan usually combines several of these, chosen around the family’s goals and the child’s level.

  • Move & Strengthen

    Supported

    Active, repeated practice of real tasks is the engine of nearly all progress.

    • Goal-directed, task-specific training means practising the real task (stand, step, climb) toward a chosen goal. The strongest and most broadly useful approach.
    • Progressive strength training: building leg strength does not make spasticity worse (a long-standing fear, now overturned) and improves strength, walking speed and balance. (meta-analysis, 27 trials) One caveat: strength on its own does not automatically turn into better walking, so pair it with task practice.
    • Home practice: short, regular, goal-focused practice woven into daily life adds the dose that makes therapy work. The amount of active practice matters more than the brand name of the therapy.

  • Walk Farther, More Easily

    Supported

    Make walking faster, steadier and less tiring through real-world practice.

    • Treadmill training, with or without body-weight support, which is among the best-ranked approaches for improving walking speed in children who can take steps (its effect on endurance is less certain).
    • Overground & functional gait training means practising real walking in real places; the backbone of mobility work.
    • Aerobic fitness improves stamina, health and participation, and helps counter the drift toward becoming less active over time.

  • Managing the Spasticity

    Supported, with care

    Medical treatments that ease the tightness itself, paired with active therapy.

    It helps to know the difference between two kinds of tightness: spasticity, meaning muscle tightness that resists quick movement, can often be eased (for example with botulinum toxin or surgery); but once a muscle becomes a fixed, shortened contracture, those tone treatments can no longer reverse it. That is why timing matters. These are decisions for a specialist team:

    • Botulinum toxin (“Botox”) injections into specific tight muscles can ease focal tightness for a few months, opening a window for active therapy and casting. It is well established for reducing tone, though its effect on day-to-day function is less certain and repeated injections raise questions about muscle health, so it is used selectively. (Cochrane review)
    • Selective dorsal rhizotomy (SDR) is a one-time spinal operation that permanently reduces leg spasticity by cutting some of the sensory nerve roots, followed by months of intensive physiotherapy. In carefully selected walking children with spastic diplegia (often GMFCS II–III, around ages 3–10), it can give lasting spasticity relief and, in well-chosen candidates, gains in walking and independence reported years later. (long-term follow-up) Because it is irreversible and, like any spinal surgery, carries risks (changes in sensation, bladder or bowel function, and later spine problems such as scoliosis), it is offered only after careful team assessment, and long-term head-to-head comparisons with intensive physiotherapy alone are still limited.
    • Serial casting uses a series of casts that gently lengthen a tight calf; effective for early, still-flexible tightness, often used after botulinum toxin.
    • Intrathecal baclofen and oral medicines are mainly for more severe, whole-body spasticity; a medical decision.

    On managing spasticity in children: NICE guideline CG145.

  • Bracing & Equipment

    Supported

    The right brace or mobility aid, matched to the specific problem.

    • Ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs) improve walking, but the type should fit the problem: a solid or leaf-spring AFO for drop-foot; a hinged AFO when the child needs to bend the ankle for sit-to-stand and stairs; a ground-reaction AFO for a flexible crouch. (AFO meta-analysis, 56 studies)
    • Walkers, gait trainers and wheelchairs: the right mobility aid for the child’s level supports independence and participation. Using a wheelchair for distance is not “giving up” on walking; it saves energy for what matters.
    • “Tone-reducing” brace features and night splints have weak evidence, so they are reasonable to question.

  • Protect the Hips & Bones

    Supported

    Keep hips in place and bones strong as your child grows.

    • Hip surveillance: regular hip X-rays on a schedule set by GMFCS level catch hip displacement early, when it is most treatable. Population-wide surveillance programs have dramatically reduced hip dislocations. (AACPDM care pathway)
    • Weight-bearing / standing, vitamin D and calcium are important for bone strength, especially in children who walk less. (For the COPB2 bone form, OPDD, plan loading together with a bone specialist.)
    • Preventing contractures: active movement, strengthening, casting and standing programs do more than passive stretching, which on its own does not prevent tightness.
Know the starting point

Understanding Your Child

GMFCS: Where They Are Now

The Gross Motor Function Classification System describes how a child moves, in five levels. Spastic diplegia is usually Levels I–III (walking, with or without aids). It helps the team set realistic goals, judge hip risk, and decide who might benefit from options like surgery. It is a starting point, not a ceiling on what to work toward.

Roughly, around ages 6–12:

  • IWalks everywhere; may find running, speed or balance harder.
  • IIWalks in most places; may hold a rail on stairs and find long distances, uneven ground or crowds harder.
  • IIIWalks with a hand-held aid such as a walker or sticks, and may use a wheelchair for longer distances.
  • IV–VMainly wheelchair mobility (less common in spastic diplegia).

GMFCS explained (CanChild)

Goals, Not “Normal”

Modern therapy sets specific goals the family chooses (for example, “walk across the kitchen safely”) and practises that exact task. Goals are tracked with simple tools (like Goal Attainment Scaling), so you can see whether therapy is working and adjust it.

What Matters Most

A simple way to keep therapy aimed at a good life, not just body mechanics: how a child does something counts more than how “normal” it looks.

Timing matters

The Early Window

Cerebral palsy and related motor problems can now be detected before about six months of age by combining a movement assessment (General Movements), a brief neurological exam (the HINE) and an MRI, long before a traditional “wait and see” diagnosis. (Novak et al., 2017) That early window is when the brain is most adaptable, so starting active, enriched, parent-delivered practice early, with programs such as GAME (Goals–Activity–Motor Enrichment), makes the most of it. (early-intervention guideline)

Parent coaching, which shows families how to build practice into everyday play, and support for caregivers’ own wellbeing are themselves evidence-based parts of good care.

Working together

Working With Your Team & Finding Help

A good therapy plan is a partnership between you, your child, and the people who help.

What Makes It Work

  • Set 2–3 concrete goals together, and revisit them.
  • Ask about dose, because meaningful change usually needs real practice volume, much of it at home, not one short clinic visit a week.
  • Measure progress with standard tools (for example the Gross Motor Function Measure, or Goal Attainment Scaling) so you can tell what is helping.
  • Build the team: physiotherapy leads here, alongside occupational therapy (daily tasks, hands, seating), speech and feeding therapy where needed, orthotics, and a doctor for spasticity and bone health.

Where to Find Help

Getting Started (US)

Free or low-cost early intervention (birth to age 3) is available in every state under IDEA Part C. A parent request or a paediatrician referral triggers a free evaluation and an individualised support plan; from age 3, services continue through the school system.

  • Early intervention: an overview
  • Find your state’s programme

Outside the US, ask your paediatrician or clinical geneticist to refer you to local child-development, early-intervention or physiotherapy services.
