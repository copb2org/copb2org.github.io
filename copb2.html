Plain-language guide

The COPB2 Gene

A clear, sourced starting point for families and individuals affected by a change in the COPB2 gene. It explains what the gene does, the conditions described so far, and what is, and isn’t, yet known. It is written to help you ask informed questions, not to replace advice from your own medical team.

Not medical advice. This page summarises published medical literature for general education. COPB2-related conditions are extremely rare and still being understood, so information here is general and may not apply to your situation, so always consult a qualified clinician or genetic counsellor about your own care.

The basics

What Is COPB2?

COPB2 (short for “coat protein complex I subunit beta 2”) is a gene that carries the instructions for making a protein called β′-COP (“beta-prime COP”). Think of a cell as a busy warehouse: proteins are constantly being made, packaged into tiny bubbles, and shipped between departments. β′-COP is one of seven parts of a “coat” (the COPI coatomer) that wraps around certain transport bubbles and carries cargo back from the Golgi (the sorting hub) to the endoplasmic reticulum (where proteins are first made). This recycling route is called retrograde transport, and it keeps the cell’s shipping system running. (NCBI Gene) (UniProt)

This job matters everywhere in the body, but it appears to be especially important in the developing brain and in bone, which is why changes in COPB2 can affect those systems. In laboratory mice, completely removing the gene stops the embryo from developing, showing how essential it is. (DiStasio et al., 2017)

One gene, a range of outcomes

What a COPB2 Change Can Mean

COPB2 makes a protein that every cell needs, and cells are sensitive to how much of it they have, so a change in COPB2 is not one fixed disease. What it does depends on two things: how many of the two gene copies are affected, and how much each change lowers the protein’s function. The map below shows the main combinations; the two named conditions, MCPH19 and OPDD, both appear on it.

This map is a simplification, not a ladder: the two conditions affect different parts of the body (the brain in one, bones in the other), are inherited differently, and why a given change leads to one rather than the other is not fully understood. Where a single copy is switched off (OPDD), the problem is simply too little protein, about half the usual amount, rather than the altered copy doing harm.

So MCPH19 and OPDD are not the two diseases of COPB2. They are the two combinations that have turned up so far, in the small number of families described to date, a handful each. They are real, well-documented conditions, but they need not be the full range of what a COPB2 change can do. As more people with COPB2 changes are described, this picture may gain detail, but that does not mean an individual’s diagnosis will change.

Here is what each of the two looks like:

Recessive · two altered copies

Primary Microcephaly 19 (MCPH19)

A severe brain-development condition present from birth, caused by inheriting an altered COPB2 copy from both parents. Features include a much smaller head and brain than expected, significant developmental delay, feeding/growth difficulty, vision problems arising in the brain, and stiff muscles. It is catalogued as OMIM #617800. Reported in only about three individuals across two families worldwide (2017 and 2023).

Dominant · one altered copy

Osteoporosis With Developmental Delay (OPDD)

A separate, generally milder condition caused by a single altered COPB2 copy, usually a brand-new (“de novo”) change not inherited from either parent. Features are childhood- or juvenile-onset weak/fragile bones (osteoporosis, with fractures in some children) plus variable, often mild developmental delay. It is catalogued as OMIM #619884, and was described in about six individuals from five families (2021).

Both conditions are allelic, meaning they involve the same gene (OMIM *606990), but they are not the same disease, and they should not be confused with one another.

A quick refresher

Genes & Variants, in Plain Terms

Your genes are an instruction manual your body uses to build and run itself. A variant (or “mutation”) is a change in those instructions. Some changes make no difference; others change how a part of the body works. With COPB2, the type of change matters:

  • A “partial” change in both copies (a so-called hypomorphic change where the gene still works, just less well) is what has been linked to the recessive microcephaly condition. The specific change reported in both families is written c.760C>T, p.Arg254Cys (often shortened to “R254C”).
  • A single “switched-off” copy (a loss-of-function change, leaving roughly half the normal amount of protein) is what has been linked to the dominant bone-and-development condition.

You may also hear the terms pathogenic (disease-causing), variant of uncertain significance (not yet fully understood), carrier, dominant and recessive. Our glossary explains these. Because so few people have been described, some databases still list the microcephaly variant cautiously while clinical reports treat it as the cause, a reminder that this science is genuinely new.

Family & inheritance

How It’s Inherited

Inheritance is often the most worrying topic for families. The two COPB2 conditions follow opposite patterns, so this is a key thing to get right with a genetic counsellor.

MCPH19 — Autosomal Recessive

A child develops the microcephaly condition only if they inherit an altered COPB2 copy from both parents. Each parent is typically a healthy carrier with one working and one altered copy. When both parents are carriers, each pregnancy has roughly:

  • 1 in 4 (25%) chance the child is affected,
  • 1 in 2 (50%) chance the child is an unaffected carrier,
  • 1 in 4 (25%) chance the child inherits two working copies.

In the first reported family the affected siblings carried two copies of the change while both parents and an unaffected sibling carried one, the classic recessive pattern. (DiStasio et al., 2017)

OPDD — Autosomal Dominant

The bone-and-development condition is caused by a single altered copy. In the reported children the change was usually de novo (new in the child and not inherited from either parent), which generally means a low chance of it happening again in the same parents’ future children (though not zero). A person who has the condition has about a 50% chance of passing the single altered copy to each of their own children. (OMIM #619884)

How people are affected

Signs & Features

Because so few people have been described, these lists reflect the published cases and may not capture the full range. Every person is different.

In MCPH19 (the Recessive Microcephaly Condition)

Reported features in affected children include: (OMIM #617800) (DiStasio 2017)

  • A head circumference well below average (by at least 3 standard deviations), present from birth and primary (the brain is small);
  • Severe developmental delay and intellectual disability;
  • Failure to thrive (difficulty gaining weight and growing);
  • Cortical visual impairment (vision difficulty arising in the brain) and crossed eyes (strabismus);
  • Spasticity (stiff, tight muscles) and, in the second reported child, seizures;
  • On brain MRI: a simplified pattern of brain folds, a thin corpus callosum, and other changes.

In OPDD (the Dominant Bone-and-Development Condition)

Reported features are usually milder and centred on bone: (OMIM #619884) (Marom et al., 2021)

  • Low bone density (osteopenia/osteoporosis) beginning in childhood or adolescence, with fractures after minor injury in some children;
  • Developmental delay that is variable and often mild, for example mild learning difficulty, a wide-based walk, or delayed motor milestones.

Whatever the diagnosis, the emotional weight on families is real: uncertainty, worry, and grief are all valid, and worth acknowledging alongside the medical facts.

Getting answers

How It’s Diagnosed

Because COPB2 conditions are so rare and their features overlap with many other genetic conditions, they are almost always found through broad genetic testing rather than a single targeted test. In the reported families the diagnosis came from exome sequencing (reading the protein-coding parts of a person’s genes), often testing the child and both parents, with the result then confirmed by a second method (Sanger sequencing). (DiStasio 2017)

Clues that may lead a doctor toward genetic testing include a very small head seen on growth charts or prenatal ultrasound, developmental delay, seizures, vision problems, or (for the bone condition) unexplained low bone density or fractures in childhood. A brain MRI and a bone density (DXA) scan can add helpful detail.

Living well

Care & Management

There are no COPB2-specific treatment guidelines yet, because the condition is too new and too rare. Care is therefore supportive and tailored to the individual, drawing on the established approaches used for similar conditions. A coordinated, multidisciplinary team usually makes the biggest difference.

For the Microcephaly Condition (MCPH19)

Care is symptom-based and may involve, for example, neurology (for seizures), nutrition and feeding support, physical and occupational therapy, vision specialists, orthopaedics for muscle tightness, and developmental/early-intervention services. The published reports focus on genetics and do not describe long-term outcomes, so prognosis should be discussed individually with your team rather than assumed from a website.

For the Bone Condition (OPDD)

The bone fragility appears treatable. In one reported child with very low bone density and repeated fractures, bisphosphonate medication (which strengthens bone) brought bone density back into the normal range. This is a single reported case, not a formal guideline, but it is an encouraging example of how the bone problem can be managed with specialist (for example endocrinology/bone) care. (Marom et al., 2021)

  • Build a care team whose members communicate with each other.
  • Keep your own records: results, letters, and questions in one place.
  • Look after the whole family’s mental and emotional health, not only the physical condition.
  • Lean on rare-disease organisations for shared experience and practical tips. See the genetic resources.
The evidence

Research & Key Papers

COPB2 was first identified as a gene in 1993, but its link to human disease is recent and based on very few patients. These are the cornerstone reports, useful to share with a doctor:

  • DiStasio et al. (2017), Human Molecular Genetics: the first report linking COPB2 to disease, describing primary microcephaly (MCPH19) in two siblings and confirming the gene’s role using mouse models. Full text (PMC) · PubMed
  • Marom et al. (2021), American Journal of Human Genetics: the report defining the separate, dominant bone-and-development condition (OPDD) and its mechanism. PubMed
  • Second-family case report (2023), European Journal of Medical Genetics: confirms the microcephaly link in a second family carrying the identical change, adding seizures and crossed eyes to the picture. PubMed

Catalogue entries: OMIM *606990 (gene), #617800 (MCPH19), #619884 (OPDD), NCBI Gene 9276. More on the Research page.

Be prepared

Questions Worth Asking Your Care Team

Appointments go by fast. Bring a written list. Here are some starting points:

  • Which COPB2 condition is this, and what is our exact gene change (the variant)?
  • What does this specific result mean for my child or family member, day to day?
  • What should we watch for, and who do we contact if it happens?
  • Which specialists should be on our team, and is there a centre experienced with this?
  • What does this mean for siblings and future pregnancies, and should family be tested?
  • Are there registries, studies, or new research we should know about?

Databases & Support

Because COPB2 is so rare, there isn’t yet a dedicated COPB2 page on the big patient sites (MedlinePlus, GeneReviews, or NORD). So we point you to the authoritative gene databases and trusted general resources. There’s also no dedicated COPB2 clinic yet, so care is usually coordinated by a clinical geneticist alongside the relevant specialists.
